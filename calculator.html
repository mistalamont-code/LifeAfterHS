Important: How Loans Really Work

When you take out a loan, your monthly payment stays the same—but where that money goes changes dramatically over time. In the early years, the majority of every payment goes toward interest, not your actual balance. This is called front-loaded interest. On a 30-year mortgage, you could be 10 years in and still owe nearly what you started with, because most of your payments went straight to the bank.

The worse your credit score, the higher your interest rate—and the more of your hard-earned money disappears into interest before it ever touches your principal. Use the calculators below to see exactly how much this costs you.

Financial Literacy Glossary

Key terms every young person should know. Understanding these words is the first step to taking control of your money.

Amortization
The process of paying off a loan through regular payments over time. Each payment is split between interest and principal. Early payments are mostly interest; later payments go mostly toward principal.
Principal
The original amount of money you borrowed—before any interest is added. When you buy a $25,000 car, the principal is $25,000.
Interest
The cost of borrowing money. It’s the fee the lender charges you for using their money. The higher your interest rate, the more you pay on top of what you borrowed.
Interest Rate (APR)
Annual Percentage Rate—the yearly cost of borrowing money, expressed as a percentage. A 7% APR on a $10,000 loan means you pay roughly $700 per year in interest.
Credit Score
A number (typically 300–850) that represents how trustworthy you are with borrowed money. Higher scores get lower interest rates. Paying bills on time, keeping balances low, and not opening too many accounts builds your score.
Front-Loaded Interest
The way most loans are structured so that you pay more interest at the beginning and more principal at the end. This means in the first years of a mortgage, very little of your payment actually reduces what you owe.
Compound Interest
Interest that earns interest. When you invest, your returns generate their own returns over time. This is why starting early matters so much—time is the most powerful factor in building wealth.
Minimum Payment
The smallest amount your credit card company requires you to pay each month. Paying only the minimum keeps you in debt far longer and costs you significantly more in interest.
Default
When you fail to make payments on a debt. Defaulting destroys your credit score and can lead to collections, lawsuits, and years of financial consequences—like Corey’s $600 credit card in the book.
Equity
The portion of an asset you actually own. If your home is worth $200,000 and you owe $150,000 on the mortgage, you have $50,000 in equity. Equity grows as you pay down principal.
Down Payment
The upfront cash you pay when buying something with a loan. A larger down payment means borrowing less, paying less interest, and often getting a better interest rate.
Secured Credit Card
A credit card backed by a cash deposit you provide. It’s one of the best tools for building or rebuilding credit because the deposit reduces the lender’s risk, making approval easier.
Debt-to-Income Ratio
The percentage of your monthly income that goes toward debt payments. Lenders use this to decide if you can afford more debt. Lower is better—aim to keep it below 36%.
Emergency Fund
Money set aside for unexpected expenses—car repairs, medical bills, job loss. Having 3–6 months of expenses saved prevents you from going into debt when life happens.
